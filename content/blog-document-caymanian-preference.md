# How to Document Caymanian Preference Compliance: A Step-by-Step Guide

*Published March 2026* | *Reading time: 11 minutes*

The November 2025 WORC rule changes transformed Caymanian preference from a principle into a documented process. Employers must now prove — with timestamps, records, and structured feedback — that they gave genuine consideration to every Caymanian applicant before seeking a work permit.

This guide walks you through the entire process, step by step, from drafting a compliant job posting to assembling your work permit application package. Follow it, and you'll have documentation that stands up to Immigration scrutiny.

---

## Step 1: Draft a Fully Compliant Job Posting

This is where compliance begins — and where many applications fail before they start. Under the [Immigration (Transition) Amendment Act, 2025](https://legislation.ky/laws/immigration-transition-amendment-2025), every job posting on the WORC Portal must include **nine mandatory fields**.

### The 9 Required Fields

**1. Job Title**
Use an accurate, industry-standard title. Don't inflate it ("Chief Innovation Architect" for what's really a mid-level developer) or obscure it ("Team Member" for an accountant). Immigration officers will compare the title to the duties and qualifications.

**2. Duties and Responsibilities**
Be specific. List the actual day-to-day tasks the person will perform. This isn't marketing copy — it's a legal document that Immigration will use to evaluate whether Caymanian applicants were qualified.

*Good:* "Prepare and review monthly financial statements, manage accounts payable/receivable, reconcile bank statements, prepare audit workpapers, file quarterly tax returns with CIMA."

*Bad:* "Handle various accounting duties as assigned."

**3. Qualifications**
List every required qualification: degrees, certifications, years of experience, specific technical skills. Be precise about what's *required* versus *preferred* — Immigration will hold you to the required qualifications when evaluating why Caymanians weren't hired.

**4. Salary**
State an exact figure or a narrow range. "CI$65,000–CI$75,000 depending on experience" is compliant. "Competitive salary" or "DOE" is not. The salary must be realistic for the role and the Cayman market.

**5. Benefits**
Itemize all benefits:
- Health insurance (specify coverage level)
- Pension contributions (specify percentage)
- Housing allowance (if applicable, specify amount)
- Travel allowance
- Annual leave (number of days)
- Any other benefits (education, professional development, etc.)

"Standard benefits package" is not acceptable. List them.

**6. Working Hours**
Specify: full-time or part-time, expected weekly hours, any shift work or irregular schedules.

**7. Job Type**
Permanent, temporary (specify duration), contract, or seasonal.

**8. Location**
The specific work location in the Cayman Islands. "Grand Cayman" is a minimum; a specific address or business district is better.

**9. Special Conditions**
Any travel requirements, physical demands, on-call expectations, or other unusual conditions of employment.

### Before You Post: The Alignment Check

Before publishing, ask yourself: *If I deny a Caymanian based on qualifications listed here, will the feedback be defensible?*

If you list "5 years of experience" as required and a Caymanian with 4 years applies, your structured feedback needs to explain why that one-year gap matters for this specific role. Make sure your requirements are genuine and defensible.

## Step 2: Post on the WORC Portal and Start the Clock

Once your posting is compliant, publish it on the WORC Portal. The **14-day consecutive advertising period** begins the moment it goes live.

### Critical Rules for the Advertising Period

- **14 calendar days, no gaps.** If the posting is removed for any reason — even briefly — the clock resets.
- **No material changes.** If you edit the salary, qualifications, or duties during the 14 days, Immigration may question whether Caymanian applicants saw the same posting as your preferred candidate.
- **Screenshot or timestamp your posting.** You'll need proof of when it went live and what it contained.
- **Keep it live even if you have applicants.** The 14 days are mandatory regardless of application volume.

### Document It

Save the following:
- Screenshot of the live posting with the publication date visible
- URL of the WORC Portal listing
- Date the posting went live
- Date the 14-day period ends

## Step 3: Track Every Caymanian Applicant

Under the new rules, you must document the outcome of **every Caymanian who applies** — not just the ones you interview. This is a departure from previous practice where only shortlisted candidates needed tracking.

### What to Record for Each Applicant

For every Caymanian applicant, maintain a record that includes:

1. **Applicant name and contact information**
2. **Date of application** (timestamp, not approximation)
3. **How they applied** (WORC Portal, careers.ky, direct email, etc.)
4. **Qualifications summary** — education, experience, certifications
5. **Status/outcome** — one of:
   - **Hired** — applicant was offered and accepted the position
   - **Offered, declined** — applicant was offered but declined
   - **Interviewed, not selected** — progressed to interview but not chosen
   - **Screened out at application** — did not meet minimum qualifications
   - **Withdrew** — applicant withdrew their application
   - **No response** — applicant did not respond to contact attempts
6. **Date of each status change**

### The Proactive Search Requirement

Simply posting and waiting for applications is no longer sufficient evidence of Caymanian preference. Immigration increasingly looks for evidence that employers **actively searched** for qualified Caymanians.

Document your proactive efforts:
- **Candidate database searches** — Did you search careers.ky's talent database? LinkedIn? Professional associations?
- **Outreach to professional networks** — Did you contact relevant Cayman-based professional groups?
- **Recruitment agency instructions** — Did you specifically ask your agency to prioritize Caymanian candidates?

On careers.ky, every talent search you run and every introduction you send is timestamped and logged — creating an auditable record of proactive effort.

## Step 4: Conduct and Document Interviews

For every Caymanian applicant who meets the minimum qualifications, you should conduct an interview. Even if you have a preferred foreign candidate, demonstrating that you genuinely evaluated qualified Caymanians is critical.

### What to Document for Each Interview

- **Date and time** of the interview
- **Location** (in-person, video call, phone)
- **Interviewer names and titles**
- **Questions asked** (use a standard set for consistency)
- **Candidate responses** (summary, not transcript)
- **Evaluation against job requirements** — how the candidate measured up against each listed qualification
- **Outcome decision** and reasoning

### Interview Best Practices for Compliance

1. **Use a standardized scorecard.** Rate each candidate against the same criteria — the qualifications listed in your job posting. This creates comparable data.

2. **Ask the same core questions.** Varying questions between candidates opens you to claims of unfair process.

3. **Evaluate against the posting, not the preferred candidate.** The question isn't "Is this person as good as our foreign candidate?" — it's "Does this person meet the requirements we listed?"

4. **Document in real time.** Notes taken during or immediately after the interview are far more credible than notes written weeks later.

5. **Have at least two interviewers.** A single interviewer's subjective assessment is harder to defend than a panel evaluation.

## Step 5: Write Structured Feedback for Every Non-Hired Caymanian

This is the most important step under the new rules — and the one most likely to cause a permit denial if done poorly.

### What "Structured Feedback" Means

Structured feedback is a written explanation of why a specific Caymanian applicant was not hired, **tied directly to the requirements listed in the job posting**. It must be:

- **Specific** — references particular qualifications, skills, or experience
- **Linked to the posting** — explains which listed requirements weren't met
- **Factual** — based on verifiable information, not subjective impressions
- **Proportionate** — the length and detail should match the candidate's qualifications (a clearly unqualified applicant needs less detail than someone who was a near-miss)

### Feedback Template

For each non-hired Caymanian applicant, structure your feedback as follows:

> **Applicant:** [Name]
> **Position:** [Job Title]
> **Application Date:** [Date]
> **Outcome:** [Screened out / Interviewed, not selected]
>
> **Assessment against job requirements:**
>
> The posting required [Requirement 1]. The applicant [has/does not have] [specific qualification]. [Explanation of gap and why it matters for this role].
>
> The posting required [Requirement 2]. The applicant [has/does not have] [specific qualification]. [Explanation].
>
> [Continue for each relevant requirement]
>
> **Conclusion:** Based on the above assessment, the applicant did not meet the following requirements: [list]. The selected candidate meets all listed requirements, specifically [brief comparison on the key differentiators].

### Examples of Compliant vs. Non-Compliant Feedback

**Compliant ✅**
> "The position requires a minimum of 7 years of experience in regulatory compliance within financial services, as stated in the job posting. The applicant has 4 years of compliance experience, primarily in retail banking, with no exposure to the mutual fund administration compliance work that constitutes 70% of this role's duties. The posting also requires CAMS certification; the applicant holds AML certification but not CAMS. The selected candidate has 9 years of regulatory compliance experience in fund administration and holds CAMS, CISI, and CFA designations."

**Non-compliant ❌**
> "The applicant did not have sufficient experience for the role."

**Non-compliant ❌**
> "After careful consideration, we determined that other candidates were better suited to the position."

**Non-compliant ❌**
> "Not a cultural fit for our organization."

### Feedback for Different Scenarios

**Applicant clearly didn't meet minimum qualifications:**
Brief but specific. "The posting requires a Bachelor's degree in Engineering. The applicant's highest qualification is a high school diploma. The posting requires 5 years of experience in structural engineering. The applicant's CV shows no engineering experience."

**Applicant was close but missing key requirements:**
More detailed. Explain specifically which requirements were and weren't met, and why the gap matters for the role.

**Applicant was qualified but another candidate was stronger:**
Most detailed. You need a comparative assessment showing specifically where the selected candidate exceeded the Caymanian applicant on posted requirements. This is where many applications fail — "stronger overall" isn't sufficient.

## Step 6: Prepare the Comparative Assessment

If you're hiring a foreign national over Caymanian applicants who met some or all of the posted requirements, you need a **comparative assessment** — a document that directly compares the selected candidate against the most qualified Caymanian applicants.

### Structure

| Requirement (from posting) | Selected Candidate | Caymanian Applicant 1 | Caymanian Applicant 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Education | [Detail] | [Detail] | [Detail] |
| Years of experience | [Detail] | [Detail] | [Detail] |
| Specific certification | [Detail] | [Detail] | [Detail] |
| Technical skill 1 | [Detail] | [Detail] | [Detail] |
| Technical skill 2 | [Detail] | [Detail] | [Detail] |

Follow the table with a narrative summary explaining, based on the posted requirements, why the selected candidate is the most qualified.

## Step 7: Assemble the Work Permit Application Package

Your application package should include all of the following, organized and clearly labeled:

### Documentation Checklist

1. **Proof of WORC Portal advertising**
   - Screenshot or printout showing the live posting
   - Dates confirming 14+ consecutive days
   - Full text of the posting with all 9 required fields

2. **Complete list of Caymanian applicants**
   - Name, application date, and outcome for each
   - Organized chronologically

3. **Structured feedback for each non-hired Caymanian**
   - Individual feedback documents (not one generic letter)
   - Each tied to specific job requirements

4. **Interview documentation**
   - Dates, interviewers, and evaluation summaries for interviewed candidates

5. **Proactive search evidence**
   - Records of candidate database searches
   - Outreach to professional networks
   - Recruitment agency communications

6. **Comparative assessment**
   - Side-by-side comparison of selected candidate vs. qualified Caymanian applicants

7. **Selected candidate's qualifications**
   - CV/resume
   - Copies of relevant certifications
   - Reference letters or verification

8. **Application form and fee**
   - Completed application form
   - CI$500 admin fee

### Quality Check Before Submission

Before submitting, verify:

- [ ] Every date in your documentation is consistent
- [ ] The job requirements in your feedback match the posted requirements exactly
- [ ] No documentation was clearly created retroactively (check metadata on digital documents)
- [ ] The salary offered to the foreign candidate matches the salary posted
- [ ] All Caymanian applicants are accounted for — none missing from the list

## Common Mistakes That Lead to Denials

Based on the first months of the new requirements, these are the most common compliance failures:

### 1. Generic Structured Feedback
The single most common cause of denial. Immigration officers are trained to identify boilerplate language. Every piece of feedback must be specific to the individual applicant and the specific role.

### 2. Missing Applicants
If WORC Portal records show 15 Caymanian applicants but your documentation only addresses 12, that's a red flag. Account for every single one.

### 3. Retroactive Documentation
Documents created after the hiring decision — especially structured feedback — are identifiable and problematic. Digital documents have creation dates. Handwritten notes have no timestamps. Build your documentation as you go.

### 4. Posting Non-Compliance
One missing field can invalidate the entire advertising period. Salary is the most commonly omitted field, followed by benefits.

### 5. Requirements Inflation
Listing requirements you don't actually need to justify rejecting Caymanian applicants is a strategy Immigration recognizes and rejects. If you require an MBA but the actual job doesn't need one, you'll be questioned.

### 6. Inconsistent Records
If your structured feedback says an applicant wasn't interviewed but your email records show an interview was scheduled, that inconsistency will be flagged. Accuracy matters.

## A Final Note on Process vs. Paperwork

The most important thing to understand about the November 2025 changes is this: **Immigration isn't just looking for documents. They're looking for evidence of a genuine process.**

You can have perfect paperwork and still be denied if the underlying process wasn't genuine. The best documentation strategy isn't about creating records for Immigration — it's about building a fair, thorough hiring process that naturally produces the documentation Immigration requires.

When you genuinely prioritize Caymanian candidates, actively search for qualified applicants, give every applicant fair consideration, and make decisions based on posted requirements — the documentation almost writes itself.

The employers who will thrive under the new rules aren't the ones with the best legal teams. They're the ones with the best hiring processes.
